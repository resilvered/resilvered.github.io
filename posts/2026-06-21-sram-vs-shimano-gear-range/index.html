SRAM 48/33 × 10-36 vs Shimano 50/34 × 11-34: The Gear Range Showdown

21 June 2026 · 8 min · Alan Chalmers

If you’re building a road bike or debating a groupset upgrade in 2026, two configurations dominate the conversation: SRAM’s 48/33 crankset with a 10-36 cassette and Shimano’s 50/34 with an 11-34. One promises wider range. The other leans on tradition. But which actually delivers where it counts — on the climb, in the sprint, and across every shift in between?

I ran the numbers. Here’s what they say.

The setups at a glance#

SRAM Shimano
Crankset 48/33T 50/34T
Cassette 10-36T (12-speed) 11-34T (12-speed)
Cassette cogs 10-11-12-13-15-17-19-21-24-28-32-36 11-12-13-14-15-17-19-21-24-27-30-34
Chainring gap 15T (145% jump) 16T (147% jump)

Right away, two design philosophies emerge. SRAM goes wide at both ends — a 10T high cog and a 36T bailout. Shimano plays it safer with an 11T start and 34T finish, relying on a bigger 50T chainring for top-end speed.

Gear ratios: the full picture#

A gear ratio is simply chainring teeth divided by cog teeth. Higher numbers mean more speed potential (harder to push). Lower numbers mean easier climbing.

The headline numbers#

Metric SRAM Shimano Difference
Lowest gear 33/36 = 0.917 34/34 = 1.000 SRAM is 8.3% easier
Highest gear 48/10 = 4.800 50/11 = 4.545 SRAM is 5.6% faster
Total range 523% 454% SRAM has 15.2% more range

This is the surprising bit: SRAM wins at both ends. It gives you an easier climbing gear and a faster top gear — all without any gaping holes in the cassette. That 523% range edge is meaningful. For context, most 2×11-speed setups sit around 450–470%.

Climbing: when the road tilts up#

The climbing equation is simple: at a given cadence, a lower gear ratio means you can ride slower without stalling. That lets you tackle steeper gradients at a sustainable effort.

Here’s what the bailout gears deliver at real-world climbing cadences (700×28c tyres, 2.136m rollout):

Cadence SRAM 33/36 Shimano 34/34 Difference
60 rpm 7.0 km/h 7.7 km/h SRAM 8.3% slower
70 rpm 8.2 km/h 9.0 km/h —
80 rpm 9.4 km/h 10.2 km/h SRAM 8.3% slower
90 rpm 10.6 km/h 11.5 km/h —

At 80 rpm — a comfortable climbing cadence — the SRAM setup lets you travel 9.4 km/h rather than 10.2 km/h. That 0.8 km/h difference doesn’t sound like much, but it translates directly into gradient tolerance. If you’re grinding up a 12% pitch and your speed drops to 9 km/h, the Shimano rider is either mashing at an uncomfortably low cadence or walking.

Real-world take: On alpine passes, loaded touring, or any climb that nudges past 10%, that 33/36 bailout is a genuine safety net. The Shimano 34/34 is no slouch — it matches what most riders had on 11-speed compact setups — but SRAM simply gives you one more gear to reach for when things get ugly.

Speed on the flat and descending#

At the other end of the cassette, the 48/10 combination puts down a 4.80 ratio — higher than you’d expect from a “sub-compact” crankset.

Cadence SRAM 48/10 Shimano 50/11 Difference
80 rpm 49.2 km/h 46.6 km/h +2.6 km/h
90 rpm 55.4 km/h 52.4 km/h +3.0 km/h
100 rpm 61.5 km/h 58.2 km/h +3.3 km/h
110 rpm 67.7 km/h 64.1 km/h +3.6 km/h

At a brisk 90 rpm, SRAM nets you an extra 3 km/h before you spin out. At 100 rpm — sprint territory — it’s nearly 62 km/h versus 58. For most riders, you’ll run out of descent or nerve before you run out of gear on either setup. But the SRAM’s 10T cog means you can hold a faster cruising speed at a more comfortable cadence on rolling terrain, especially if you like to sit around 85–95 rpm.

The 10T trade-off: A 10-tooth cog does come with marginally higher drivetrain friction than an 11T — the chain has to articulate around a tighter radius. In practice, SRAM’s Flattop chain and the XDR driver body were designed around this, and independent testing (Zero Friction Cycling, CeramicSpeed) puts the efficiency loss at well under 1 watt at typical power outputs. You won’t feel it. But it’s worth acknowledging.

Gear steps: how smooth is the shift?#

Both cassettes are 12-speed, so neither feels gappy by modern standards. Here are the step percentages between adjacent cogs on the big ring:

2→3 8.3% 7.7%
3→4 7.7% 7.2%
4→5 13.3% 6.7%
5→6 11.8% 11.8%
6→7 10.6% 10.5%
7→8 9.5% 9.5%
8→9 12.5% 12.5%
9→10 14.3% 11.1%
10→11 12.5% 10.0%
11→12 11.1% 11.8%

Two things stand out:

  1. SRAM’s tight top end. The single-tooth jumps at 10→11→12→13 mean the four fastest gears are closely spaced — perfect for maintaining cadence in a fast paceline or rolling terrain. Shimano gives you 11→12→13→14, which is also good but starts one tooth slower.

  2. SRAM’s mid-range gap. The 13→15 jump (13.3%) is SRAM’s biggest step in the cruising zone. Shimano has a smoother progression through 13→14→15. If you spend a lot of time in that 30–38 km/h range at a fixed cadence, Shimano gives you one more intermediate ratio.

The remaining mid-to-low steps are comparable, with both cassettes spacing out to sensible gaps as you move into climbing territory.

Front shifting: chainring gap matters#

SRAM’s 48/33 has a 15-tooth gap (145% jump). Shimano’s 50/34 has a 16-tooth gap (147% jump). Smaller gap = easier front shifts, all else equal. In practice, both modern groupsets handle front shifts cleanly — SRAM’s Yaw derailleur design eliminates the need for trim positions entirely — but the slightly tighter SRAM gap is marginally friendlier when you need to dump from big to small ring on a sudden ramp.

Practical overlap#

Both setups have significant overlap between big and small rings, so you’re not constantly cross-chaining. With SRAM, the 33T small ring combined with the 10-11-12-13 top cogs gives you ratios of 3.30 down to 2.54 — these overlap neatly with the 48T ring on the 15-21T range. You can stay in the big ring for most flat-to-rolling terrain and only drop to the small ring when gradients exceed 3–4%.

Shimano’s overlap is similar: the 34T ring with 11-12-13-14 cogs (3.09–2.43) overlaps with the 50T ring’s 17-21T range.

Which one is for you?#

Pick SRAM 48/33 × 10-36 if:#

  • You ride in steep terrain regularly — alpine passes, 15%+ ramps, loaded bikepacking.
  • You value range above all else — the 523% span covers everything from a 25% climb to a 70 km/h descent without compromise.
  • You want one bike that does everything — fast group rides, solo epics, and the occasional gravel detour.
  • You’re running a SRAM AXS ecosystem and want the cleanest integration (fully wireless, no cables, the 10T needs an XDR freehub body anyway).

Pick Shimano 50/34 × 11-34 if:#

  • You prefer traditionally spaced gearing and don’t want the 10T cog’s slightly tighter chain wrap.
  • Your riding is predominantly flat to rolling and you want the extra intermediate ratio in the 13→14→15 range for fine cadence control at cruising speed.
  • You’re already on a Shimano HG freehub (11-34 fits the standard 11-speed HG body) — switching to SRAM means a new XDR freehub or wheelset.
  • You appreciate the slightly bigger 50T ring for aesthetic and marginal-efficiency reasons, and the 34/34 low gear is enough for your local climbs.

The verdict#

These are both excellent modern drivetrains, and you won’t suffer on either. But if you forced me to pick:

The SRAM 48/33 with 10-36 is the more versatile setup. It gives you a meaningfully easier climbing gear and a faster top gear in one package — something that shouldn’t be possible on paper but is. The 523% range is the widest available on any road 2×12 drivetrain, and it achieves that without the gappy feel of a 1× mullet setup. For the rider who wants to climb Alpe d’Huez one day and hammer the valley road the next, without ever thinking about gear limitations, it’s the clear winner.

That said, if you’re a flatland rider who never sees double-digit gradients and you value the subtle refinement of a slightly more progressive mid-cassette, the Shimano 50/34 × 11-34 remains a beautifully judged setup. It’s the evolution of the compact crankset formula that’s served road cyclists for decades — just refined to 12-speed perfection.

Either way, we’re living in a golden age of gearing. The days of grinding a 39/25 up a 10% grade are, mercifully, behind us.

All calculations assume 700×28c tyres with a 2.136m rollout. Your actual speeds will vary with tyre size, pressure, and road surface. Gear inches calculated against a nominal 28.3" wheel diameter.
